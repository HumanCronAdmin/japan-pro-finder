# Divorce in Japan as a Foreigner: What You Need to Know

If you're a foreign resident going through a divorce in Japan, you're facing a system that can feel stacked against you. The language barrier alone is enough to make the process overwhelming. This guide breaks down what you actually need to know.

## The Custody Problem

Japan has historically operated under a **sole custody** system after divorce. In practice, this has meant that the parent who physically has the children at the time of separation often ends up with custody -- and in most cases, that's the Japanese spouse.

For foreign fathers especially, this has been a painful reality. Many have reported being told by Japanese lawyers to simply "endure" the situation.

### The 2026 Reform: Joint Custody Is Now Possible

As of **April 1, 2026**, Japan's amended Civil Code formally allows **joint parental authority after divorce** for the first time. This is a significant change, but practical enforcement is still developing. If you're going through a divorce now, make sure your lawyer is up to date on these changes.

## Types of Divorce in Japan

| Type | What It Means | When It's Used |
|------|--------------|----------------|
| **Kyogi Rikon** (Consensual) | Both parties agree and sign papers | Simplest, no court needed |
| **Chotei Rikon** (Mediation) | Family court mediator helps negotiate | When you can't agree directly |
| **Saiban Rikon** (Litigation) | Full court trial | Last resort, can take 1-2 years |

Most international divorces end up in mediation or litigation because of disagreements over custody and assets.

## Asset Division: What You're Entitled To

Under Japanese law, marital assets are generally split 50/50 regardless of who earned more. This includes:

- Savings accumulated during marriage
- Real estate purchased during marriage
- Retirement benefits accrued during marriage
- Investments (NISA, stocks, etc.)

**Watch out for:** If your spouse controls the household finances (a common arrangement in Japan), document all accounts before initiating divorce proceedings.

## Finding the Right Lawyer

Not all lawyers are equal when it comes to international divorce. You need someone who:

1. **Speaks English fluently** (not just "English available")
2. **Has handled international custody cases** before
3. **Understands the Hague Convention** (critical if one parent wants to leave Japan)
4. **Is listed on embassy referral lists** (US, UK, Canadian embassies maintain lists)

## If Your Spouse Took the Children

This is unfortunately common and terrifying. Here's what to do immediately:

1. **Do NOT leave Japan.** If you leave, it becomes exponentially harder to get custody.
2. **Contact your embassy.** They maintain lists of family law attorneys and can provide consular support.
3. **File for mediation (chotei) at the family court** as soon as possible. Delays work against you.
4. **If your spouse plans to take the children abroad**, the Hague Convention may apply. Contact a Hague Convention specialist immediately.
